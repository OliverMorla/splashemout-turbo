# VIBE

Splash 'Em Out should feel like a bright, clean, high-trust local laundry platform: practical, fresh, friendly, and built for fast action.

## Core Feeling

- Clean without becoming sterile.
- Local without becoming old-fashioned.
- Helpful without becoming wordy.
- Modern without feeling like generic SaaS.
- Friendly without becoming cute.
- Commercially capable without losing residential warmth.

The site should feel like walking into a well-run, attended laundromat where the next step is obvious.

## Visual Taste

- Use Tailwind neutral as the dominant material for backgrounds, text, borders, panels, and forms.
- Use watery blue as the main brand signal, mint as the cleanliness signal, and citrus only for approved promotions.
- Favor crisp utility, real facility proof, visible pricing, and map/location structures.
- Keep radii modest, section structure clean, and CTAs obvious.
- Let photography prove the service: machines, attendants, folded orders, delivery bags, and location exteriors.
- Use motion only to clarify service flow or add polish.

If the screen reads as generic blue SaaS, beige wellness, purple AI, luxury fashion, or detergent-ad overload at first glance, it is off-vibe.

## UX Taste

- Location before lifestyle.
- Pricing before persuasion.
- Scheduling before storytelling.
- Proof before polish.
- Commercial quote clarity before sales language.

Users should always be able to find a location, schedule pickup, call, view pricing, or request a bid without scrolling through vague brand copy.

## Copy Taste

- Use plain service names.
- Put important numbers near the relevant action.
- Name what the customer gets and when.
- Keep commercial copy operational.
- Use local place names naturally.
- Make forms literal and easy to complete.

Good Splash 'Em Out copy is useful first and charming second.

## Anti-Vibe

Splash 'Em Out must not feel like:

- A generic laundromat template.
- A faceless national pickup app.
- A luxury lifestyle laundry brand.
- A discount coupon site.
- A vague cleaning company.
- A stock-photo family-service page.
- A cluttered small-business site with every detail in the nav.

Do not use hidden pricing, vague CTAs, oversized decorative bubbles, fake local SEO pages, generic stock heroes, or commercial claims that operations have not verified.

## Design Test

Before shipping a screen, ask:

1. Can a mobile visitor act within a minute?
2. Is the closest service or location path obvious?
3. Are price, minimum, turnaround, or quote expectations clear where needed?
4. Does the page prove cleanliness and dependability?
5. Does the design feel specific to laundry in Central Kentucky?

If the answer is not clearly yes, make the interface more direct, more local, and more useful.
